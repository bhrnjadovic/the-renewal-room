The Science

Understanding PRF

What is PRF?

Platelet-Rich Fibrin (PRF) is a second-generation platelet concentrate first developed by Dr. Joseph Choukroun in 2001. Unlike its predecessor PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma), PRF is produced without the use of anticoagulants or any external additives.

The result is a completely autologous biomaterial — a dense fibrin matrix rich in platelets, white blood cells, and growth factors. This natural fibrin scaffold acts as a sustained-release system, delivering concentrated healing factors to the treatment site over an extended period of 10 to 14 days.

How PRF Works

A small volume of the patient's blood is drawn and immediately placed into a centrifuge. Through controlled centrifugation at low speed, the blood naturally separates into distinct layers.

Without anticoagulants, the natural coagulation cascade begins during centrifugation, forming a three-dimensional fibrin network that traps platelets, leukocytes, and circulating stem cells within its architecture.

This fibrin matrix provides a slow, sustained release of growth factors over 10–14 days — far longer than the brief burst release seen with traditional PRP. This prolonged delivery creates an optimal environment for tissue regeneration, collagen synthesis, and cellular repair.

Bioactive Molecules

Growth Factors in PRF

PDGF

Platelet-Derived Growth Factor

Stimulates cell proliferation, chemotaxis, and angiogenesis. Plays a critical role in wound healing by recruiting mesenchymal stem cells and promoting collagen synthesis at the injury site.

TGF-β

Transforming Growth Factor Beta

Regulates extracellular matrix production, modulates the immune response, and promotes tissue remodelling. Essential for scar-free healing and long-term structural tissue repair.

VEGF

Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor

Drives the formation of new blood vessels (angiogenesis), improving blood supply to healing tissues. Critical for delivering oxygen and nutrients to regenerating cells and supporting tissue viability.

EGF

Epidermal Growth Factor

Stimulates epidermal cell growth, proliferation, and differentiation. Accelerates wound closure, enhances skin texture, and is a key driver of the rejuvenation effects seen in aesthetic PRF treatments.

IGF

Insulin-Like Growth Factor

Mediates cell growth, differentiation, and survival. Works synergistically with other growth factors to enhance bone regeneration, cartilage repair, and muscle recovery following injury or treatment.

Where Science Meets Practice

Clinical Applications

Aesthetic Medicine

PRF is used for facial rejuvenation, under-eye treatment, skin texture improvement, and scar revision. The fibrin matrix stimulates collagen and elastin production for natural, lasting results without synthetic fillers.

Orthopaedics

Regenerative injection therapy for osteoarthritis, tendinopathies, ligament injuries, and chronic joint pain. PRF promotes genuine tissue repair rather than merely masking symptoms with cortisone or painkillers.

Hair Restoration

PRF injections stimulate dormant hair follicles, extend the anagen (growth) phase, and increase follicular density. Clinical studies show significant improvement in hair count and thickness after multiple sessions.

Dentistry

Originally developed for oral and maxillofacial surgery, PRF accelerates bone graft healing, socket preservation after extraction, and soft tissue regeneration. It remains a cornerstone of regenerative dentistry.

Wound Healing

PRF membranes are applied directly to chronic wounds, diabetic ulcers, and surgical sites to accelerate closure and reduce scarring. The concentrated growth factors create an optimal microenvironment for rapid tissue repair.
